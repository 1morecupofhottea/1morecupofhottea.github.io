Collecting transcribed speech data is slow and expensive. For a language like Khmer, where the speech tech community is small and annotators are scarce, you have to get creative.

One approach I've used extensively: generate synthetic training samples using a Khmer Text-to-Speech engine, then mix them with real recordings to train ASR models.

Here's how to do it — and where it can go wrong.

## Why TTS augmentation works at all

A well-trained TTS system produces speech that shares acoustic characteristics with real speech: the same phoneme inventory, the same prosodic patterns (roughly), the same recording conditions (roughly). An ASR model trained partly on TTS audio learns acoustic–phoneme mappings that transfer to real speech.

The key phrase is "well-trained TTS system." A bad TTS engine produces artifacts that teach your ASR model the wrong things.

## The workflow

**Step 1: Build a text corpus**

You need Khmer text — lots of it. Sources I've used:
- Khmer Wikipedia dump
- News article corpora
- Crawled web text (carefully cleaned)

Filter for variety: sentence length, vocabulary, domain (news, conversational, technical). Don't just take the first N sentences from a sorted file.

**Step 2: Generate audio**

Send text through the TTS engine in batches. For each sentence, I generate 2–3 variants with slightly different speed and pitch parameters to add diversity.

Watch for:
- Sentences that are too long (TTS quality degrades on long inputs)
- Numbers, dates, abbreviations — these often get mispronounced
- Foreign words (English loanwords in Khmer text): TTS may handle these inconsistently

**Step 3: Quality filter**

Not all TTS output is usable. I filter by:
- Duration (too short = likely truncated, too long = likely repeated)
- Spectral flatness (flags artifacts)
- A small held-out set of sentences I listen to manually to calibrate the filters

**Step 4: Mix with real data**

I treat TTS samples as lower-weight training examples. In practice, sampling 1 TTS sample for every 2–3 real samples works well. Too much TTS and the model overadapts to the synthetic distribution.

## What TTS augmentation is good for

- **Vocabulary coverage**: real corpora have long tails of rare words. TTS can cover vocabulary that doesn't appear in your limited real-speech corpus.
- **Phoneme coverage**: if certain phoneme combinations are rare in your real data, generate sentences specifically containing them.
- **Consistent transcriptions**: TTS-generated audio has perfect ground-truth transcriptions — no annotation errors.

## What TTS augmentation won't fix

- Naturalistic prosody and conversational speech patterns
- Code-switching (most Khmer TTS handles Khmer only)
- Speaker diversity — TTS is typically 1–5 voices
- Background noise robustness — TTS output is clean

For these, you still need real data collection.

## Bottom line

TTS augmentation is a force multiplier for low-resource ASR, not a replacement for real data. Used carefully, it can cut your real-data requirement substantially while improving vocabulary coverage and reducing WER on unseen terms.
